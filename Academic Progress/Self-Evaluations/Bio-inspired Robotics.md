In hindsight, this course might have permanently changed the trajectory of my studies, and possibly my life, depending on the outcome of future events.

Before this quarter, I felt pretty rootless in terms of where I wanted to take my studies. It felt like I was just doing upper division credit work for the sake of it, like none of it was really sticking. I knew I wanted to go into cybernetics, but given that Evergreen does not offer courses in it, I kind of just had to take whatever I felt like would get me there. After clicking so well with Native Marine Resource Management the previous quarter, I thought agriculture and natural resource management was where I was going. And then, I took this course, and everything changed.


### The Robot


The main crux of this course was the robot group project, which was the polar opposite experience of my group project in Neural Networks happening at the same time. My group worked cohesively and we all thought on the same wavelength, which was fantastic to experience. It looked for a time like we were really going to struggle with getting the robot to move, but it all worked out in the end better than I think any of us were expecting. It was a massive sigh of relief to see so much work pay off like that.



### Vehicles


While a comparatively smaller part of the course, only encompassing a reading and a lab, reading Valentino Braitenberg's Vehicles had an indelible impact on my brain. In it, Braitenberg, a cybernetician himself, analyzes the various systems within a rhetorical organism that make up biological processes--fear, anger, food-seeking, and what Stafford Beer would call 'algedonic processes' (pain and pleasure). It was like watching everything in my brain suddenly realign in service of knowing what I needed to do now. It has inspired me to write a book, and to look more into biological cybernetics as a field of study that previously wasn't on my radar. Reading this book gave me a renewed sense of purpose, and I'm forever thankful to my professors for assigning it.
