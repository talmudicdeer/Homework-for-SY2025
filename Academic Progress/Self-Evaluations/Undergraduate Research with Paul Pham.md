I joined this research project about halfway through the quarter, which was several weeks later than intended. As a consequence, I was playing catch-up from the beginning, and at several points it threatened to come completely off at the wheels. However, I was able to make a near-total recovery, managing to just barely hit the minimum threshold for full credit.



### Battlecode


The main crux of this research project was Battlecode, one of MIT's many creations. Specifically, it was familiarizing ourselves, and solving many problems, with the Battlecode API and many, many AI player files. Prof. Pham had me and the two other students in the research group tackle these problems through mob and pair programming. This had mixed results; I feel like if we had had more students than just three, it would've worked out a lot better, and put less pressure on us. A lot of shortcomings came from the three of us being unable to have our schedules align just right. (This has been corrected by Prof. Pham for next quarter, and I think the results are going to be a lot better.) I actually really liked the Battlecode portions of the course and thoroughly enjoyed 'driving', or doing the actual programming with direction from others. I requested to drive almost every time because I knew that I needed the experience and knew next to nothing about Java, which meant I was almost an appendix when it comes to giving direction. It was really special writing and testing our work constantly.



### Other Work in Java


Eventually, the utility of our work in Battlecode started having diminishing returns; with the new rules and API slated for release next month, the purpose of our work in Battlecode--to familiarize ourselves with it in preparation for optional participation in the competition next quarter--was pretty much complete. For the last two or three weeks of the quarter, we worked on a different project: mob-programming a Battleship game, something that excited me quite a bit (I loved playing Battleship as a child). We also were assigned completing the project on our own, which was quite fun.



### Other Work in Game Studies


Our various projects and coding assignments were supplemented with philosophical readings on game design and play, which is a topic that interests me to the extreme. We also read various articles and papers on the philosophical underpinnings of pair and mob programming, which helped me, and the other students, understand why we did it more. Those readings are also what made me think that the very small size of our group kind of hampered pair programming's more beneficial aspects.
