### Introduction: In Which Our Heroine Braves the Mighty Fortress of Go

I have very rarely felt like I've gone on a fantasy-type quest by simply going to class before, but there's a first time for everything, I guess. Between being frequently sick, again, and having to learn a new programming language from the beginning, the first half or so of this quarter felt like an unsurmountable climb.

For starters, it wasn't exactly clear what us software devs were supposed to *do* for a couple of weeks, and it's not like that particular front improved considerably; I more just adapted to it. Being strictly software meant being second fiddle to hardware the entire quarter, as that was the side of the project pretty much everyone else was dipping their toes into, and the few others working on software more or less felt like they had everything under control as it was. The amount of work that needed to get done was not commensurate with the volume of people we had doing software. So, it became patently obvious that my job was more or less academic, and to piggyback on the others so I could contribute *something* to the project.

### Go's Dungeons: The Challenges

I'd say that the biggest challenge this quarter was trying to understand the API we had built, or trying to functionally onboard myself to a boat that didn't really have a use for me. And while both of those were challenges, the biggest one, it turns out, was the goddamn flu.

I got sick three separate times this quarter: once over winter break that ended up making me miss the first day of class; again during W2-3, and again W7-8, this time knocking me out for the full weeks. And each time I got sick, like I found during fall quarter, it put me farther and farther behind.

I had several things not going my way this quarter that I did last; one is that I was sick during this quarter's extended weekend, knocking out valuable time for me to catch up. Another, and probably the worst, is that I was not working with papers and projects that could be paused until I got better, but a live project that people were going to be working on whether I was well or not. They were not going to pause for me every time I got sick. So whenever I *did* get sick, it meant I lost more and more time to be of any use to people.

### Assailing Go: What I Learned

Actually, I learned quite a lot. Ths was helped by Go resting right in the optimal spot of the learning curve.

I had a somewhat similar relationship to Go as I did to Rust last quarter: I did not have the highest opinion of it. But whereas with Rust that was me looking up at it, due to an extremely intimidating culture surrounding it, as well as the code itself feeling overengineered, Go I looked down on. I have a sort of implicit distrust of Google projects in general, as well as attempts at making code "more readable" for humans. (Quite worth a paper of its own, but I think it's us that should learn how to speak the computer's language and not the other way around.)

Be that as it may, much to my surprise (and possibly slight chagrin) our 12 Go files were... extremely easy to understand. I didn't even need to look up Go's documentation to figure it out. Right there, plain as day, were the structs that Shawn and I demonstrated to Prof. Weiss.

As the quarter progressed, the cohesiveness of the programs only got better. At no point during the quarter was spaghetti a real concern, and when it was, we simply found a solution to "do more with less". (I don't know how much detail I should go into here, since a fair amount of what we actually did concerned making the program more secure.) Even though I had never touched the program before, nothing Shawn talked about in our huddles was foreign to me; it was all concepts I had learned in Data Structures & Algorithms, and Go didn't hide these concepts at all.

Week 9 was when it really all came together, both for me and for the program. After weeks of everyone being sick and asymmetrical development, we finally assembled our hardware, and Shawn and I were able to show our complete program to Prof. Weiss. We were ready to ship, barring a few additional changes Shawn wanted to make.

### Conclusion: In Which Our Heroine Returns Exhausted, but No Worse For Wear

This program was an adventure, one I wish I had been able to participate in from the start, as the idea of it was my co-creation. I also wish I had been healthy for the duration of it, and it wasn't for lack of trying. But I am substantially happy with how everything turned out in the end. I feel that I really did learn something completely new that will imminently useful in this new chapter of my life.
