### Part 2: Book Questions

**Chapter 1.1**

**1. What are the four zones common to all neurons?** For this question, I related the four zones of the neuron to similar concepts in cybernetics.

- The **input zone**, where neurons collect instructions at the dendrites. These instructions are transmitted via synapse, extremely small gaps between neurons. This corresponds to, well, the input of a feedback loop, where the loop gathers information that's being transmitted in from a source, whether that be an individual cell or a gargantuan international complex.
- The **integration zone**, where the neuron's cell body combines the information recieved from the input zone with already existing information to determine whether to send a signal through the output. In second-order cybernetic schemes, like Beer's viable system model that was directly modeled off the functions of the brain, the integration zone is quite literally the lynchpin of the entire model and appears at every scale, for without it the system can't function.
- If a neural cell body decides that an instruction must be sent to another neuron, the instruction is then sent down the axon towards the cell tail. This is called the **conduction zone**. In the viable system model, this is displayed through Beer's many arrows and jaunty shapes as he describes how information from the morass gets processed through the system.
- Instructions are then sent out from the axon terminals in the **output zone** to other neurons, also through synapses. This corresponds to the output of a feedback loop, where an instruction might change and is then used to change behavior.

**2. Describe the three main components of the synapse. What are some of the specialized structures found on each side?** The presynaptic membrane on the side of the axon terminals, the postsynaptic membrane on the side of dendrites, and the infinitesimally small synaptic cleft, through which instructions pass from cell to cell. The presynaptic axon terminal, as well as the membrane, are filled with synaptic vesicles that are full of neurotransmitter. On the postsynaptic side, the membrane is covered with neurotransmitter receptors that capture neurotransmitters released across the synaptic cleft.

**3. What special properties does myelin have?** Fatty sheaths formed by the glial cells that boosts processing speed of information through neurons.


**Chapter 1.2**

**1. Name the major divisions of the peripheral nervous system. What general function does each part perform?** The somatic nervous system, which handles communication between the brain and major muscles and sensory organs, and the autonomic nervous system, which handles communication with visceral systems (systems made up of internal organs).

**2. Why does the human cortex look so lumpy on the outside?** The folds allow more brain matter in less space.

**3. What is special about the pre- and postcentral gyri?** The precentral gyri is also called the primary motor cortex and is critical for motion control. The postcentral gyri is also called the primary somatosensory cortex and recieves somatosensory information from the entire body.


**Chapter 2.1**

**1. How is the action potential propagated along the axon?** Actively and regenerated along the axon.

**2. What is saltatory conduction?** Conduction as it occurs in myelinated axons, where the action potential traverses between nodes of Ranvier.

**3. Why do action potentials move only in one direction (towards the pre-synaptic terminal)?** Due to voltage-gated sodium ion pathways.

**4. What is meant by spatial and temporal summation?** Spatial summation happens when a decision is made by multiple presynaptic neurons and an instruction is sent from said neurons to a postsynaptic neuron simultaneously; temporal summation is the same process but executed by only one presynaptic neuron.

**5. What are EPSPs and IPSPs?** Both are postsynaptic potentials, or PSPs. EPSPS, or excitatory PSPs, temporarily depolarize the postsynaptic membrane, increasing the likelihood of it firing an action potential, while IPSPs, or inhibitory PSPs, are a temporary hyperpolarization and decrease that same likelihood.


**Chapter 2.2**

**1. Once an action potential reaches the pre-synaptic terminal, what ion enters to initiate the fusion of the synaptic vesicles?** Calcium ions.

**2. How does the release of neurotransmitter initiate an action potential in the post-synaptic neuron?** Neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the dendritic end of neurons, typically gated ion channels. This flood of ions either depolarizes or hyperpolarizes a neuron, leading to the increased or decreased likelihood of an action potential.
