### Part 2: Questions

**1. Describe the pathway from the left visual field to the right side of the brain.** Left visual field information goes to the right side of the left retina and the left side of the right retina. Information is passed to rod and cone cells, and from there to the optic nerve. At this point information segregates and left visual field information crosses over the optic chiasm to the right side. Nerve fibers are then combined into the right optic tract. This then synapses directly to the right LGN in the thalamus.

**2. You are testing a patient who has had their optic chiasm cut down the midline. You put a divider between her two eyes and show the images in the figure below. What would you predict this person would perceive? You can draw the shapes or circle them in the figure.** The heart and the lightning bolt.

**3. Explain what a person would see after staring at the red circle for about 30 seconds. Why does this occur?** The circle would start to change color to a cyan-ish shade. This is because of photoreceptor fatigue.

**4. What mechanism accounts for some color blindness?** Alterations in the balance of cone cells.

**5. How do each of the following contribute to the perception of sound in a mammal?**
  **a. Pinnae:** Localizes sound
  **b. Tympanic membrane:** Converts sound from a wave to a mechanical vibration
  **c. Ossicles:** Amplifies sound
  **d. Cochlea:** Converts mechanical vibrations into electric impulses

**6. What is the relationship between frequency and pitch?** Frequency is an objective and measurable phenomenon. Pitch is an organism's subjective interpretation of frequency.

**7. Explain how the cochlea decodes complex sound waves.** Through the basilar membrane, and specifically which parts of the membrane vibrate; the base handles higher frequencies, and the the apex handles lower frequencies.

**8. Describe the gating of the K+ cells of the hair cells in the cochlea.** They're gated mechanically through physical movement, a process called mechanoelectrical transduction.

**9. What are the three types of deafness?** Sensorineural, conductive, and mixed sensorineural-conductive.

**10. How can you locate where a sound is coming from if you can’t see? That is, how does the brain process information from the two ears to allow for localization of sounds?** Interaural time difference and interaural level difference, spectral cues, and reverberation/attenuation of sound.

**13. Describe what happens when you rotate your head to the left in terms of the VOR?** Endolymph flows against the hair cells, causing depolarization in the left ear, and flows away from the hair cells, causing hyperpolarization, in the right ear.

**14. From your answer to #13, describe what would happen if you were rotated counterclockwise on a stool with your eyes closed for several minutes. Explain what happens inside the semicircular canals and the eye movements.** The inertia of the initial spinning causes the endolymph in the canals to lag behind and the eyes to dart back and forth but resetting to the left. This is called left-beating nystagmus. As you get used to it, eventually the endolymph evens out and you stop experiencing nystagmus.

**15. After being rotated on the stool for several minutes, you are stopped. What would your eye movements look like? Explain why this would occur.** Post-rotary nystagmus, which is one of the most unpleasant sensations I've ever felt. Your eyes do this because inertia hasn't caught up to reality yet.
