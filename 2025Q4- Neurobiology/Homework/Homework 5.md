### Part 2: Learning and Memory

**Define learning and memory.** Learning is the process of acquiring new information, behaviors, etc. through practice, study, and/or experience. Memory refers to both the ability to learn and catalog information for repeated retrieval, and the catalogued information itself.

**Provide an example of each of the following:**
  **a. Positive reinforcement:** Congratulating someone for completing a task
  **b. Negative reinforcement:** Wake-up alarms
  **c. Positive punishment:** Corporal punishment
  **d. Negative punishment:** Grounding a child

**How many items in a list can the average person recall in terms of STM?** About 3-5.

**Why does ‘chunking’ help with STM recall?** Makes things easier to record by associating small fragments of information with something.

**Define sensitization and habituation.** Sensitization is the increase in response to repeated exposure to a stimulus, and habituation is the opposite: a decrease in response to repeated exposure to a stimulus.

**What is the mechanism for sensitization of the gill-withdrawal reflex in Aplysia? For habituation?** For habituation the mechanism is repeated harmless touch -> decreased Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx at sensory terminal -> less glutamate released -> weaker motor activation. For sensitization, the mechanism is shocking the tail -> serotonin released by interneuron -> high cAMP & PKA inside sensory terminal -> K<sup>+</sup> channels closed -> longer action potential -> high Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx -> mass glutamate release -> strong motor activation.

**Detail how the circuitry in Aplysia changes during short-term and long-term sensitization.** During short-term sensitization, the physical circuitry does not change, but the strength of existing synapses is strengthened. During long-term sensitization, the physical circuitry permanently expands to accomodate the new stimuli.

**Where is the hippocampus located?** In the medial temporal lobe

**What is Long Term Potentiation and what hippocampal neurons are involved?** LTP is a stable, enduring increase in synaptic effectiveness following repeated, strong stimulation. It involves the perforant and mossy fiber pathways of the hippocampus as well as the Schaffer collaterals.

**Describe the differences between the NMDA and AMPA receptors involved in LTP.** AMPA receptors are active during moments of low- and high-level activity, whereas NMDA receptors are only involved in high-level activity and are gated with a magnesium ion to regulate this.

**How does opening of the NMDA promote long term potentiation?** By allowing the influx of Ca<sup>2+</sup> ions into the postsynaptic cell.

### Part 4: Neural Development

**Compare and contrast the embryonic and mature NMJ. Include information about the following:**
  **a. Distribution of ACh receptors:** AChRs in the embryonic NMJ are spread out and not dense at all, but densify with age until we get to the mature NMJ, where they're highly localized and dense.
  **b. Innervation of muscles at both stages:** Embryonic muscle fibers are inenrvated by multiple axons, coming from multiple neural inputs. As the NMJ ages, these excess connections are shed.
  **c. Functional differences of the ACh-receptors:** The embryonic gamma subunit is replaced by the mature epsilon subunit, fundamentally altering how the receptors process.

**Describe the experiments Rita Levi-Montalcini used to demonstrate that NGF is a molecule secreted by target cells.** She ablated the limb cells from chick embryos. Using silver-staining, she was able to visualize developing nerve cells really quite well. She found that while neurons initially still reached out to the missing limb, quite the opposite happened as the embryo developed, resulting in massive cell death and neurodegeneration. This proved that neurons didn't "recruit" new cells, but used a heretofore unknown molecule to continue maintenance of the neural network.

**PC12 cells allowed researchers to study the effects of NGF. Why were scientists unable to study the effects of NGF using cultured DRG neurons and what qualities of PC12 cells made it possible?** It was nearly impossible to establish a control group with cultured DRG neurons since DRG neurons die without NGF, and to study the effects of NGF they'd need a control group without it. PC12 cells, meanwhile, can survive just fine without NGF, making the establishment of a control group possible.
