## 1976 Paper


### Abstract/Introduction

Since this paper lacks a 'true' abstract, I'll be annotating here key terms, the main research question posed in the introductory paragraph, and asking what the study is about and why it's important, as well as some notes about the authors.

> ionic channel associated with the acetylcholine (ACh) receptor at the neuromuscular junction of skeletal muscle fibres

Not really a 'term', but definitely the key concept of the paper and it appears in the first sentence.

> Nevertheless, the properties of individual channels are still unknown, as previous studies were concerned with average population properties.

The authors are clearly teeing up the crux of the study here by highlighting the main concept being investigated. Ion channels were only formally discovered a few years before this paper was published, and their actual construction and operation were still a mystery. The authors are showing a new and revolutionary technique for detecting infinitesimally small electrical currents that resulted in them describing the function of ion channels for the first time.

> Clearly, it would be of great interest to refine techniques of conductance measurement in order to resolve discrete changes in conductance which are expected to occur when single channels open or close.

Why is this important? I think about the quote from Albert Michaelson (misattributed to Lord Kelvin for well over a century at this point) that argued there was nothing new to be discovered in the existing models of physics but that "discoveries" would come from the sixth decimal place. While incorrect (and Kelvin himself argued against this idea, going so far as to highlight areas of physics he thought would see revolutions), it is true that new methods of more precise measurement can reveal things we previously had no idea existed. This is just as true when it comes to physiology and biology as it is chemistry and physics.


### Methods

> The key to the high resolution in the present experiments lies in limiting the membrane area from which current is measured to a small patch, and thereby decreasing background membrane noise.

Always a surefire way to improve measurements: eliminate extraneities.

> This is achieved by applying closely the tip of a glass pipette, 3-5 μm in diameter, on to the muscle surface, thus isolating electrically a small patch of membrane.

Here, the authors start to describe their clamp-patch technique, which will win them the Nobel Prize 15 years after this paper was published. (Nobody has ever accused the Nobel Committee of being expeditious.) The next sentence, which I won't be quoting in full but is worth a mention, says that this method has been tried previously with larger pipette diameters to muscles as well as the neural components of mollusks.

> fire-polished edges

Fire-polishing is a method of treating glass so as to completely smooth it, ideal in scientific settings.

> is filled with Ringer's solution

Ringer's solution is comprised of various salts dissolved in water.

> Its interior is connected to the input of a virtual-ground circuit, which clamps the potential inside the pipette to ground and at the same time measures current flowing through the pipette, that is, through the patch of membrane covered by the pipette to a fixed value by a conventional two-microelectrode clamp. Thus, voltage clamp conditions are secured across the patch of membrane under investigation. Since current densities involved are very small, a simple virtual ground inside the pipette is preferable to more complicated arrangements for stabilising potential described previously [footnote here cites Neher & Lux, 1969].

This is the focal point of the patch-clamp process. Using a virtual circuit allows a ton more flexibility at the tiny scale the authors are working with, as anything more complex would make balancing the setup more difficult and also introduce extraneities that they were trying to avoid.

> The dominant source of background noise in these measurements was the leakage shunt under the pipette rim between membrane and glass.

This paragraph discusses their method for solving the leakage problem, which their solution for was to treat the muscle with collagenase and protease, which digested tissues and enabled closer contact between the membrane and the pipette. This process came with the significant risk of making the membrane more and more fragile and at greater risk of damage from the pipette, however it did _not_ change the sensitivity of the fibers to ACh or influence ACh-induced conductance. A good solution, albeit a highly risky one that threatened to scupper testing if not handled extremely delicately.

> All experiments were carried out on the extrasynaptic region of denervated hypersensitive nerve fibres.

You could classify this sentence as part of the results, since the next sentence starts off the results section of this paper, but it's literally describing a part of the method, so I'm putting it here. Of note here is _denervated_ fibers. Using denervated fibers made it easier to localize specific channel changes thanks to the lower density of ACh. (When ennervated, ACh is densely packed at the neuromuscular junction, making it much more difficult to single out individual channel uptakes and downtakes.)
