### Part 2: Questions

**Chapter 5.3**

**1. What is a motor unit? How are motor units recruited when picking up a heavy object? (hint: think about how tension is sensed by the muscles).** A motor unit consists of a single motor neuron and the related fibers connected to it. They're recruited via Henneman's size principle, from smaller ones to larger. For a heavier object, the larger motor units are going to be recruited.

**2. How does motor information travel from the motor cortex to the spinal cord and to the muscles? Does it cross the midline? If so, where?** Via pyramidal cells in the motor cortex and down the anterior horn of the spinal cord. It does cross the midline, at the medulla.

**3. What types of sensory information do muscle spindles and Golgi tendon organs provide? Describe how the muscle spindle fiber works.** They provide information on stretch and tension. The Golgi tendon organs monitor muscle tension. When the Golgi tendon organ and muscle spindle are unexcited, the muscle is relaxed; when the Golgi tendon organ is excited and the spindle is not, the muscle is contracted; when the tendon organ and spindle are both excited, the muscle is stretched.


**Chapter 6**

**1. Briefly describe the signaling pathways in the taste cell for each of the 5 basic tastes.** They broadly fall into two categories: salty and sour are more iontropic in receptor orientation, while sweet, bitter, and umami are more metabotropic.

Sweet: Triggered by sugars/sugar-like substances binding to a heterodimer of T1R2 and T1R3 proteins.
Bitter: Compounds that we are programmed to detect as toxic (whether or not they actually are) bind to a garden variety of T1R2 proteins.
Umami: There's two different signals for umami, at least; one is a variant of the glutamate receptor, and the other is a heterodimer of T1R1 and T1R3 proteins.
Sour: the more straightforward of the two ionotropic receptors. The dominant theory at this moment is that sour-sensitive taste cells contain a modified ion channel protein sensitive to hydrogen ions, which acids release (this is what makes them acids, after all), and which feature an inward flow of protons to depolarize the cell.
Salty: probably the most complex taste receptor. We're not entirely sure how it works, exactly. The most obvious aspect is how taste cells disposed to salt detect it: through sodium ion channels. Sodium ions from salty food enter these channels, depolarize the cell, and release a neurotransmitter. What's less clear is how our taste cells handle the now freed chlorine ions when salt is dissolved.

**2. In what ways are the signaling pathways for olfaction similar to taste? How are they different?** They're both dependent on GPCRs for detection and transmit information to the brain via depolarization of ion channels. The biggest difference between the two is scale. Humans have exponentially more olfactory receptors than taste receptors, leaving far more room for differentiation and particularities.

**3. What is a GPCR and how do they work in the cAMP signaling pathway? The IP<sub>3</sub> pathway?** GPCRs are a type of membrane receptor. In the cAMP pathway, a ligand binds to it, causing the receptor to activate a stimulatory G-protein. Once the receptor binds to an inhibitory G-protein, the process ceases. In the IP<sub>3</sub> pathway, when a ligand binds to a GPCR, it activates a different G-protein from those used in the cAMP pathway.

**4. Where do afferents of taste and olfaction project to in the brain?** Taste is routed to the thalamus, whereas olfaction goes straight to the primary cortex.

**5. What is meant by olfactory fatigue?** Olfactory fatigue happens over time as the olfactory receptors adapt to constant smells so as not to cause sensory overload, leading to some smells just not being detected over time.

**6. Why are humans not as good at olfaction as other organisms?** Because vision became more evolutionarily important.


**Chapter 7**

**1. Explain the ‘Dark Current’ in the photoreceptor cells.** A constant stream of positive ions into photoreceptors when there's no light.

**2. Where does the lens focus the image on the retina? What is the term for images that are focused in front of or behind the retina and how are they corrected?** The lens focuses the image onto the fovea. Images that are focused in front of the retina are myopic and corrected with concave lenses. Images that are focused behind the retina are hyperopic and corrected with convex lenses.

**3. What is odd about the arrangement of the layers of the retina?** That photoreceptors are placed at the back, rather than the front, like one would assume.

**4. What is the fovea and what type of photoreceptors are most abundant there?** The fovea is a small, highly sensitive depression in the back-center of the macula. The photoreceptors there are cones; at the center, it's *only* cones.

**5. Why/how do bipolar cells respond differently to the same neurotransmitter released by the photoreceptors? How does it change the response in the ganglion cells in the presence and absence of light?** Bipolar cells release less neurotransmitter in the presence of light and more in darkness, but are really reacting to changes in light level. In darkness, the off-center ganglion cells recieve larger quantities of glutamate, causing them to fire rapidly, and causing the on-center ganglion cells to stay quiet. The inverse is true in the presence of light.
